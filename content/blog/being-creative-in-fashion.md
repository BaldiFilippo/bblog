Perhaps the easiest way to begin is by accepting that **we’re not all creatives**.

Jordan Peterson frames it clearly: creativity isn’t evenly distributed. It requires intelligence, divergent thinking, and particular personality traits that not everyone possesses. What took me longer to understand is that creativity is never neutral.

It’s a gift that opens doors but also a curse.

When I first started my creative journey, I don’t think I even knew what being “creative” meant. I only knew that creativity existed around me. My dad was an engineer, my sister was an architecture student, and my aunt could draw effortlessly; the bloodline kept going. Still, I had no idea what creativity would look like in this industry. I just knew it fascinated me.

I was 18, freshly arrived in Milan, completely unaware of what the city could offer. I hated it. I didn’t want to stay. I barely knew how to use the metro. Milan felt cold, overwhelmed, and not mine. At that point, I had graduated in Hospitality, Tourism, Culinary, and Pastry. As a kid, I was convinced I wanted to be a chef. Even now, my parents still say I would’ve been perfect for it. But that certainty didn’t survive Milan. Did it? Heh.

Like many teenagers, I was ambitious in my own way, though mostly I was playing by my parents’ rules. Being forced to live in a city I hadn’t chosen, I was sure of one thing: I wanted to leave. No doubts. So I looked for a job, something stable, something that would let me leave. At some point, I told myself: Milan won’t last forever. So I kept living my life in what I thought was a cursed city.

Then I got accepted as a sous chef at Nobu.

 was shocked. At the time, I didn’t even realize how famous it was. I showed up, worked my first day, and that was it. That was the moment I knew cooking wasn’t for me. It felt as if all those years of studying, wanting it, dreaming about it… were suddenly gone. Just like that.

I didn’t know why. The city? The building? The pressure? The environment?
Maybe all of it. I quit after the first day. I told them I wouldn’t come back. And strangely, I wasn’t scared. I knew I could easily find another job in the same field. I had always believed I was a good cook because my aunt was, because my mum was. They both have big hearts; they express love through food, through feeding people. That generosity shaped me.

But quitting carved something else into me, too. It cracked me open. It forced me to look elsewhere. And, quietly, it felt like something I had always wanted, to be open to something different. Something better. Something completely unknown.

And that’s where my creative journey started.

I didn’t know what I wanted to do. So I took the risk of stepping into unexplored territories. I had no money, no stable job, and no connections. I didn’t even know what networking meant. Those questions kept looping in my head.


![](/images/blog/being-creative-in-fashion/01.webp)

And yet, somewhere in the middle of that confusion, I realized something important: I was already in a position where I could choose who I wanted to become. The uncertainty didn’t paralyze me: it forced me to think critically. To strategize. To look back and notice that creativity had always been part of my life. I just hadn’t learned how to develop it yet. And maybe those things weren’t problems after all.

Because, in some strange way, I already had everything I needed to move toward where I wanted to be, even if I didn’t know exactly what that place looked like yet. I took a leap of faith and just went with it.

That was one of the best decisions I’ve ever made in my life.

![](/images/blog/being-creative-in-fashion/04.webp)

The feeling behind that choice still resonates deeply with me, especially in relation to my project, The Unknown Season.

The Unknown Season is a project that feels very close to me. It was born from a moment of transition, when certainty hadn’t arrived yet, but something inside me knew I was moving in the right direction.


![](/images/blog/being-creative-in-fashion/03.webp)


It comes from a place I know well, “the in-between”. That moment where you’ve outgrown who you were, but you’re not fully sure who you’re becoming yet. I’ve lived that phase personally, creatively, geographically. This project is my way of giving shape to it.

My team and I built The Unknown Season around two figures: the Newcomer and the Local.

They're not characters in conflict. They’re mirrors. One is arriving, observing, and trying to understand the rhythm of a place. The other already belongs, but still remembers what it felt like to be new. I see myself in both.

For me, this isn’t just a fashion story.

It’s about creatives, especially those who exist quietly behind the scenes. The assistants, the stylists, the photographers, the people holding everything together without always being seen. I wanted to honour that invisible work, because that’s where I come from too. Visually, we kept things restrained on purpose. Silence, space, stillness. I didn’t want noise or excess. We wanted the images to breathe, the way you do when you’re standing in a new chapter of your life, unsure but open.

This project reflects a season I believe many creatives go through: when you’re building without guarantees, when you don’t fully belong yet, when uncertainty isn’t weakness, but potential.

Unknown Season is not a phase everyone enters, but a territory only some are drawn to. It belongs to those compelled to step into uncertainty, to confront what is undefined, and to remain there long enough for meaning to emerge. Creativity, in this sense, is not comfort; it is exposure.

It is the willingness to stand at the edge of chaos and attempt to shape it.
