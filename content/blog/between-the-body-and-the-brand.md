## The Image Before the Object

In contemporary fashion, the brand image often precedes the garment itself. By the time a customer touches the fabric, they have already formed an emotional relationship with the house — through campaigns, casting, spatial design, and the curated silence of a flagship store. Visual identity is not applied on top of fashion. It is woven into every decision made before the collection reaches a body.

This is what separates a label from a brand. A label produces clothes. A brand constructs a world you want to inhabit.

### Identity Lives in Proportion

The most effective fashion identities are not built on logos — they are built on proportion. The width of a lapel, the drop of a shoulder, the hem that hovers exactly three centimetres above the floor: these are the grammar of a house. A trained eye can identify a Margiela garment without reading a single label.

When working on creative direction, the first question is never *what does this look like?* It is *how does this feel to stand inside?* Identity begins with the body and radiates outward — into the room, into the image, into the archive.

### Casting as Communication

Nothing communicates a brand's values faster than who is wearing it. Casting is one of the most undervalued tools in creative direction. The models, the faces, the way they walk — all of this is visual language, and it must be consistent with the clothes they carry.

A fragile garment carried by a powerful presence creates tension. Tension creates memory. Memory creates desire.

The choice to cast non-models, older faces, or unconventional body types is never neutral. It repositions the brand in relation to beauty, to power, to time. Every season, this conversation continues — and the creative director is responsible for every word of it.

### The Edited Store

Physical retail is the most honest expression of a brand's identity. You cannot hide in three dimensions. The temperature of the light, the density of the rail, the scent that greets you at the door — these elements communicate more than any campaign.

The most powerful stores are the ones that feel slightly under-stocked. Scarcity is a design choice. It says: *we believe the thing is worth pausing in front of.* An overcrowded rail suggests panic. A single garment, lit correctly, on a painted concrete floor, suggests certainty.

Certainty is what every customer is buying when they buy fashion.

### The Continuity Question

One of the most challenging aspects of creative direction is maintaining continuity across seasons without calcifying into repetition. The customer must always recognise the house, but never feel they have already seen this collection.

This requires building a visual vocabulary rich enough to sustain variation. Certain elements — a specific shade, a recurring silhouette, a preferred textile — act as anchors. Around these anchors, everything else can move.

The archive is not nostalgia. It is a working document.

### What Identity Costs

Building a coherent visual identity takes longer than most clients want to admit. It requires saying no to opportunity, to trend, to the instinct to respond to everything happening in the market. Identity is defined as much by what a brand refuses to do as by what it does.

The discipline of refusal is perhaps the most radical act in an industry that celebrates abundance. To say *this is not us* — and to mean it, every season, regardless of what is selling elsewhere — is the foundation of any identity worth building.
